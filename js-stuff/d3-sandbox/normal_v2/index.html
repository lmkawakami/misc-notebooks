Visual Interpretations

This interactive page illustrates a fraction of the complexity of disease testing and some of its tradeoffs, focusing on the statistical/probabilistic side.
DISCLAIMER: It's a simplification, just a toy model with some knobs, made by an electrical engineer without any medical knowledge.

Populations Distribution:

  • The green curve represents the distribution of healthy individuals, and the orange curve represents the distribution of sick individuals
  • The green hatched area represents the healthy individuals that tested negative (True Negatives)
  • The blue area represents the healthy individuals misdiagnosed as sick (False Positives)
  • The orange hatched area represents the sick individuals that tested positive (True Positives)
  • The red area represents the sick individuals misclassified as healthy (False Negative)
  • Poor tests present wide overlaps between the two distributions
  • When the threshold is increased, the proportion of false negatives goes down, but the false negatives go up.

Confusion Matrix:

  • Ideally, the bottom-left cell (false negative) and the top-right cell (false positive) tend to zero

Roc

  • The ROC curve shows how well the test can differentiate sick individuals from healthy ones
  • When the test can't differentiate sick from healthy (poor test), the ROC curve becomes a straight diagonal line from (x=0, y=0) to (x=1, y=1)
  • When the sick and the healthy can be totally segregated (perfect test), the ROC curve goes straight from (x=0, y=0) to (x=0, y=1) to (x=1, y=1) like an "upside-down L"
  • Poor tests show ROC curves closer to the diagonal, and superior tests show ROC curves closer to the "upside-down L"
  • Every threshold value is represented by a point on the Roc curve. Dragging the threshold line you can see the point "moving"

Bayesian views:

  • The Bayesian views show the non-intuitive behavior of testing and illustrate why a single measure isn't sufficient to corroborate how good the test is
  • The "precise-test" present 95% of precision, but only 42% of sensitivity (less than half of the sick individuals are correctly diagnosed as sick)
  • The "sensitive-test" present 95% of sensitivity, but only 30% of precision (70% of the individuals diagnosed as sick are healthy)
  • The only difference between the simulated "precise-test" and "sensitive-test" is the threshold value

Visualization of Real-life Disease Testing:

To illustrate a real-life situation data from a published paper was reproduced, showing how two different tests can differ. Clicking on the "simulate" buttons below, you can simulate the conditions reported on the journal and see the differences between the two kinds of tests and what happens when some parameters are changed. Assuming that the model and distributions are correct, the Panbio test can differentiate sick individuals from healthy individuals better than the Standard-Q test. Reducing the Panbio threshold to 50 its sensitivity and NPV are enhanced, reaching almost 100%, without degrading other indicators.
Apparently, both tests prioritized specificity over sensitivity.

*Obs: "Sick μ" and "Healthy μ" were inverted because the model expects "Sick μ" to be greater than "Healthy μ" which was not the case for the measurements from the journal, but the inversion doesn't impact the analysis.

"Standard Q" and "Panbio" test data source: Diagnostic accuracy of two commercial SARS-CoV-2 antigen-detecting rapid tests at the point of care in community-based testing centers
